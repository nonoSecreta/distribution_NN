Biorthogonal Systems

Biorthogonal systems provide a structured way to represent elements of finite-dimensional spaces, playing a crucial role in the paper's proof of the representer theorem by enabling the construction of stable right inverses for the operator $$R_m$$.

Motivation

In functional analysis and variational problems, it's often necessary to construct specialized operators that satisfy specific properties. Biorthogonal systems provide a powerful tool for this purpose, particularly when dealing with finite-dimensional spaces.

In the context of the paper, biorthogonal systems are used to construct a stable right inverse of the operator $$R_m$$, which is a critical step in proving the representer theorem. This construction enables the authors to decompose the native space as a direct sum, characterize the structure of solutions, and ultimately show that single-hidden layer neural networks solve the variational problem.

Mathematical Definition

Let $$\mathcal{N}$$ be a finite-dimensional space with dimension $$N_0$$. A biorthogonal system for $$\mathcal{N}$$ is a pair $$(\phi, p) = \{(\phi_n, p_n)\}_{n=1}^{N_0}$$ where:

  • $$\{p_n\}_{n=1}^{N_0}$$ is a basis of $$\mathcal{N}$$
  • $$\phi = (\phi_1, \ldots, \phi_{N_0})$$ is a vector of functionals, where each $$\phi_n$$ belongs to the continuous dual space $$\mathcal{N}'$$
  • The functionals and basis elements satisfy the biorthogonality condition: $$\langle \phi_k, p_n \rangle = \delta[k - n]$$ for $$k, n = 1, \ldots, N_0$$, where $$\delta[\cdot]$$ is the Kronecker delta

The key property of a biorthogonal system is that any element $$q \in \mathcal{N}$$ has a unique representation:

$$q = \sum_{n=1}^{N_0} \langle \phi_n, q \rangle p_n$$

In the paper, the authors use a biorthogonal system for the null space $$\mathcal{N}_m$$ of the operator $$R_m$$. This null space consists of polynomials of degree less than $$m$$, and is finite-dimensional (Lemma 19 in the paper).

Using this biorthogonal system, the authors construct a stable right inverse operator $$R_{m,\phi}^{-1}$$ that maps measures in the Radon domain to functions in the native space, satisfying:

  1. $$R_m R_{m,\phi}^{-1} \psi = \psi$$ (right-inverse property)
  2. $$\phi(R_{m,\phi}^{-1} \psi) = 0$$ (boundary conditions)

The kernel of this operator has the form:

$$g_{m,\phi}(x, z) = r_z^{(m)}(x) - \sum_{n=1}^{N_0} p_n(x)q_n(z)$$

where $$r_z^{(m)}(x) = \rho_m(w^T x - b)$$ and $$q_n(z) = \langle \phi_n, r_z^{(m)} \rangle$$.

Example

To illustrate the concept of biorthogonal systems, let's consider the case of $$m=2$$, where the null space $$\mathcal{N}_2$$ consists of linear functions:

$$\mathcal{N}_2 = \{q(x) = u^T x + s : u \in \mathbb{R}^d, s \in \mathbb{R}\}$$

This space has dimension $$N_0 = d+1$$. A natural basis for this space is:

$$p_1(x) = 1, p_2(x) = x_1, p_3(x) = x_2, \ldots, p_{d+1}(x) = x_d$$

To construct a biorthogonal system, we need functionals $$\phi_n$$ that satisfy $$\langle \phi_k, p_n \rangle = \delta[k - n]$$. One possible choice is:

$$\phi_1(f) = f(0), \phi_2(f) = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}(0), \ldots, \phi_{d+1}(f) = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_d}(0)$$

With this biorthogonal system, any linear function $$q(x) = u^T x + s$$ can be uniquely represented as:

$$q(x) = \langle \phi_1, q \rangle p_1(x) + \sum_{i=1}^d \langle \phi_{i+1}, q \rangle p_{i+1}(x) = s \cdot 1 + \sum_{i=1}^d u_i \cdot x_i$$

In the proof of the representer theorem, the authors use a specific biorthogonal system derived from the measurement operator. They identify a subset of $$N_0$$ measurements that are linearly independent with respect to $$\mathcal{N}_m$$, and use these to construct the biorthogonal system.

This construction enables them to split the optimization problem into a part involving the null space and a part involving the Radon domain, ultimately showing that the solution is a single-hidden layer neural network with at most $$N - N_0$$ neurons (plus a polynomial term from the null space).
